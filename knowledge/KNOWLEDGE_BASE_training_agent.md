# 2. SCIENTIFIC FOUNDATION: SOMATOTYPE VALIDITY

## 2.1 Origin and Debunking

William Herbert Sheldon (1898-1977) introduced the -mesomorph-endomorph classification in the 1940s as **constitutional psychology** — linking physique to personality, temperament, and criminality. This was not a simple body-type taxonomy but a pseudoscientific theory embedded in the eugenics movement.

**Key evidence against Sheldon's model:**

- Eysenck (1970): Controlled research did not support Sheldon's personality-physique correlations
- Rafter (2007, _Criminology_): Classified constitutional psychology as pseudoscience comparable to phrenology
- Stephen Jay Gould (_The Mismeasure of Man_, 1981): Condemned conclusions as heavily influenced by subjective interpretation
- Barbara Honeyman Heath (Sheldon's own research assistant): Publicly accused him of data falsification

**Scientific consensus**: The personality-physique link is entirely discredited. No scientific organization supports constitutional psychology.

## 2.2 The Heath-Carter Method: What Survives

Heath & Carter (1967, _American Journal of Physical Anthropology_) reformulated the system using **10 objective anthropometric measurements** (skinfolds, circumferences, bone widths). This version:

- Abandons ALL psychological claims
- Explicitly recognizes somatotype as **changeable** with diet, training, and aging
- Is taught by ISAK (International Society for the Advancement of Kinanthropometry) in 85 countries
- Serves as a **descriptive tool only** — not predictive, not prescriptive

**Critical limitations** (Wilmore, 1970): Only moderate relationship between endomorphy and body fat percentage; virtually no relationship between mesomorphy and lean mass. Campa et al. (2025): Considerable intra-category variability — individuals with the same somatotype can have very different body compositions.

## 2.3 What Somatotype Labels Actually Describe

The popular use of " " describes a cluster of observable traits — smaller appetite, higher NEAT, narrower skeletal frame, lower baseline muscle mass — that make weight gain _harder_ but never _impossible_. These traits are:

- **Not fixed** — Stone et al. (2024): Mesomorphy scores increased 10.65% over a 41-day athletic season
- **Not predictive of training response** — No study links somatotype to hypertrophy rate under controlled conditions
- **Not a basis for dietary prescriptions** — No RCT has ever validated "eating for your body type" (Stukas et al., 2024 was observational only; Precision Nutrition's 100,000+ client dataset found no benefit)

## 2.4 Agent Decision Rule

> **When the user references " " or "body type"**: Acknowledge the terminology as useful shorthand for current phenotypic traits. Do NOT treat it as a fixed category or limitation. Frame all recommendations around modifiable variables (caloric intake, training stimulus, recovery) rather than constitutional type.

# 3. SCIENTIFIC FOUNDATION: GENETICS OF BODY COMPOSITION

## 3.1 Heritability Estimates

| Trait                     | Heritability                  | Key Source                                       |
| ------------------------- | ----------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------ |
| Height                    | 80-84%                        | GIANT consortium                                 |
| BMI                       | 47-90% (median 0.75)          | Elks et al. 2012, 88 twin studies, 140,525 twins |
| Body fat percentage       | 47-78%                        | HERITAGE study; DXA twin studies                 |
| Fat distribution (WHR)    | 30-60%                        | GIANT consortium, 694,649 individuals            |
| Endomorphy (Heath-Carter) | 28-32%                        | Peeters et al. 2007, 803 Flemish twins           |
| Mesomorphy (Heath-Carter) | 82-86%                        | Peeters et al. 2007                              |
| y (Heath-Carter)          | 67-70%                        | Peeters et al. 2007                              |
| Muscle mass               | 55-80%                        | Multiple family studies                          |
| Muscle fiber composition  | ~45%                          | Vincent et al. 2007                              |
| Resting metabolic rate    | ~40% (adjusted for lean mass) | Bouchard et al. 1989                             |
| VO2max baseline           | 40-60%                        | HERITAGE; twin meta-analyses                     |
| VO2max training response  | ~47%                          | HERITAGE                                         |

**Critical insight**: Endomorphy (fat component) is the MOST modifiable (h² ≈ 28-32%), mesomorphy (musculoskeletal) is the LEAST modifiable (h² ≈ 82-86%) but still changes significantly with training.

## 3.2 Key Genes

**ACTN3 R577X** ("speed gene"): XX genotype (18% of Caucasians) = complete deficiency of alpha-actinin-3 in fast-twitch fibers. Per-allele OR for elite sprinters = 1.4, but explains only 0.92% of sprint time variance. Overwhelmingly outweighed by training for non-elite populations.

**Myostatin (MSTN)**: Negative regulator of muscle growth. Knockout mice develop ~2x normal muscle mass (McPherron & Lee, 1997, _Nature_). Extremely rare loss-of-function mutations in humans (Schuelke et al., 2004, _NEJM_). Common variants show inconsistent associations.

**FTO** (rs9939609): First obesity gene identified via GWAS. Two risk alleles confer ~3 kg additional weight through increased food intake, NOT metabolic rate (Cecil et al., 2008, _NEJM_). Effect attenuated by ~27% in physically active individuals.

## 3.3 The "Fast Metabolism" Myth

Johnstone et al. (2005, _AJCN_, 150 adults): **63% of BMR variance explained by fat-free mass**, 6% by fat mass, 2% by age. Only ~26% unexplained. One standard deviation = 5-8% of average BMR = ~200-300 kcal/day difference between 96% of similar-sized people.

**NEAT is the real variable**: Levine et al. (1999, _Science_): During 1,000 kcal/day overfeeding for 8 weeks, NEAT changes accounted for **10-fold differences in fat storage** (r = 0.77). Some individuals unconsciously increased expenditure by up to 700 kcal/day. Levine (2004): NEAT varies by up to 2,000 kcal/day between similar-sized people.

## 3.4 Training Response Variability

**HERITAGE Study** (Bouchard et al., 481 sedentary Caucasians, 98 families): After 20 weeks of standardized aerobic training, mean VO2max gain was ~19%, ranging from ~0% to 40-50%. Heritability of training response = 47%.

**Hypertrophy response** (Bamman et al., 2007): Three clusters among 66 subjects after 16 weeks — "extreme responders" (+58% myofibrillary hypertrophy), "modest" (+28%), "non-responders" (0%). BUT: Montero & Lundby (2017, _Journal of Physiology_) showed "non-responders" DO respond to **higher training volume** — it's a higher stimulation threshold, not a ceiling.

## 3.5 Epigenetic Plasticity

Lindholm et al.: 3 months of single-leg endurance training produced DNA methylation changes on **4,919 CpG sites** and 4,076 differentially expressed genes in the trained leg vs. untrained leg of the same individual.

Rönn et al.: 6 months of endurance training modified methylation on **16,470 CpG sites** in adipose tissue, affecting 18 obesity candidate genes and 21 type 2 diabetes genes.

**Key principle**: The environment doesn't just "modulate" genetics — it literally reprograms gene expression at the molecular level.

## 3.6 Universal Weight Gain Under Overfeeding

Bouchard twin overfeeding study (1990, _NEJM_): 12 pairs of identical twins overfed 1,000 kcal/day for 84 days. Mean weight gain = 8.1 kg, range 4.3-13.3 kg. **Every single participant gained weight.** The Sims Vermont Prison studies (1968-1973) confirmed: all inmates gained 9-11 kg when overfed.

## 3.7 Agent Decision Rule

> **When the user expresses doubt about ability to gain**: Reference Bouchard 1990 — universal weight gain under sufficient surplus. Frame difficulty as caloric intake problem (smaller appetites, higher NEAT), NOT an inability to respond to training.

# 4. SCIENTIFIC FOUNDATION: FEMALE-SPECIFIC PHYSIOLOGY

## 4.1 Women Build Muscle at the Same Relative Rate as Men

**Roberts, Nuckols & Krieger (2020, JSCR)**: Meta-analysis of 10 studies using identical protocols. Hypertrophy effect size difference = **0.07 ± 0.06 (P = 0.31)** — statistically indistinguishable.

**Refalo et al. (2025, PeerJ)**: 29 studies, Bayesian methods — confirmed relative hypertrophy is similar between sexes. Absolute differences slightly favor males only in upper body.

**Hagstrom et al. (2020, Sports Medicine)**: First meta-analysis exclusively on female RT adaptations — confirmed large improvements in both strength and hypertrophy.

**Practical rate for untrained women**: ~0.5-1.0 kg muscle per month during first year (0.23-0.45 kg/month is the conservative evidence-based range). McMaster 111-study review (2025): ~1 kg lean mass in 10 weeks is a realistic benchmark for novice women.

## 4.2 Compensatory Anabolic Mechanisms in Women

Women produce 15-20x less testosterone than men (15-70 ng/dL vs 300-1,000 ng/dL). Yet achieve similar relative hypertrophy via:

1. **Growth Hormone**: ~3x higher production than men (Burman et al., 2000, _JCEM_). Kraemer et al. (2021): Resistance-trained women show higher resting GH and higher IGF-1 at all time points.

2. **Unique IGF-1 response**: Nindl et al. (2019, _Frontiers in Endocrinology_): Women experience sustained increases in free IGF-1 isoforms that men do not produce.

3. **Estrogen is directly anabolic**: Lowe, Baltgalvis & Greising (2010, _ESSR_) meta-analysis of HRT studies: postmenopausal women on estrogen replacement had ~5% greater muscle strength and ~10% greater specific force. Estrogen activates satellite cell proliferation via ERα, suppresses protein catabolism during follicular phase.

4. **Local mechanisms dominate**: Colenso-Semple et al. (2024, _ESSR_): Acute postexercise rises in testosterone, GH, and IGF-1 are "neither sufficient nor necessary for stimulating MPS or maximizing hypertrophy." Muscle growth is driven by mechanical tension and intramuscular signaling, not circulating hormones.

## 4.3 Menstrual Cycle and Training

**Studies favoring follicular-phase training:**

- Sung et al. (2014, _SpringerPlus_): Significantly greater muscle diameter and type II fiber size increases
- Wikström-Frisén et al. (2017, _JSMF_): Only follicular-training group achieved significant increases in leg lean mass
- Reis et al. (1995, _IJSM_): 33% maximal strength increase with menstrual periodization vs 13% with constant frequency

**BUT**: Colenso-Semple et al. (2023, _Frontiers in Sports and Active Living_, umbrella review): Evidence quality is low. Few studies verified cycle phase with hormones. Many assumed standard 28-day cycles.

**Agent recommendation**: Train consistently throughout the cycle. Optionally increase volume slightly during follicular phase (days 1-14). Allow more recovery during late luteal if symptoms warrant. **Consistency > cycle optimization.**

## 4.4 Female Fat Distribution Mechanism

Pedersen et al. (2004, _JCEM_): Estradiol increases antilipolytic α2A-adrenergic receptors in subcutaneous (NOT visceral) adipocytes via ERα. This makes gluteofemoral fat in premenopausal women more resistant to lipolysis than abdominal fat.

Steiner & Berry (2022, _Frontiers in Endocrinology_): Estrogen reduces adipogenesis in visceral fat via mTOR and PPARγ inhibition.

**Practical implication**: When a young premenopausal woman enters caloric surplus, estrogen directs fat preferentially to hips, thighs, buttocks. This is metabolically protective and aligns with the subject's aesthetic goals. After menopause, this pattern shifts toward android/abdominal storage.

**For the subject specifically**: Her existing gynoidal fat pattern will be maintained and enhanced. New fat will go to hips/thighs/glutes. Combined with muscle development underneath, this creates the aesthetic she wants.

## 4.5 Health Benefits of Moving from BMI 18.5 to 21.5

Being underweight (BMI <18.5) carries: menstrual irregularity/amenorrhea, reduced estrogen impairing satellite cell function, elevated cortisol, weakened immunity, increased depression risk, higher all-cause mortality (The Lancet 2009, 900,000 adults).

Mallinson et al. (2013, _JISSN_): Two amenorrheic women who increased caloric intake saw **restored menstrual function within 23 and 74 days** respectively, with normalization of T3, leptin, and resting energy expenditure.

## 4.6 Agent Decision Rules

> **When user reports feeling "too thin" or frustrated with lack of curves**: Validate the concern medically (BMI 18.5 = borderline underweight with documented health risks). Frame weight gain as both aesthetic AND health intervention.

> **When user worries about "getting fat"**: Explain estrogen-mediated fat distribution — new fat will go to hips/thighs/glutes, not abdomen. Combined with muscle underneath, the result is shape, not shapelessness.

> **When user asks about testosterone for muscle building**: Explain compensatory mechanisms (GH 3x, estrogen anabolic, IGF-1 isoforms). Women do NOT need testosterone supplementation for hypertrophy.

(2014, _J Nutr_): Even distribution (30/30/30g) → **25% higher 24h MPS** than skewed (10/20/60g) in women. Leucine threshold: ~2.5g per meal (Witard et al., 2014), achieved with ~25-30g quality protein.

## 5.4 Nutrient Timing

Schoenfeld, Aragon & Krieger (2013, _JISSN_), meta-analysis of 23 studies: After controlling for total protein intake, **post-workout timing effects disappeared entirely**. Total daily intake >> timing.

**Practical approach**: Have a protein source within 2-3 hours around training. Don't stress exact timing.

## 5.5 Strategies for Low Appetite ("Hardgainer" Hacks)

**Scientific basis**: Self-identified hardgainers typically overestimate caloric intake by 20-50% in tracking studies. The problem is almost always under-eating, not over-metabolizing.

| Strategy            | Mechanism                                              | Implementation                                                                               |
| ------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------ | -------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Liquid calories     | Less satiety than solid food (DiMeglio & Mattes, 2000) | Daily homemade shake: banana + oats + whey + tahini + whole milk = 600-800 kcal              |
| Calorie-dense foods | More energy per gram/volume                            | Whole milk, nuts, nut butters, dried fruit, granola, full-fat dairy, olive oil on everything |
| Scheduled eating    | Override inadequate hunger signals                     | 5 meals/snacks at fixed times, don't wait for hunger                                         |
| Drink AFTER meals   | Prevent stomach volume displacement                    | No fluids 15 min before or during meals                                                      |
| Dense foods first   | Prioritize calories before fiber fills stomach         | Eat protein/starch first, vegetables last                                                    |
| Larger plates       | Visual portion normalizing                             | Use dinner plates for all meals                                                              |

## 5.6 Adjustment Rules

- **Weight gain < 0.25 kg/week for 2 consecutive weeks** → Add 200 kcal/day
- **Weight gain > 0.75 kg/week** → Reduce by 150 kcal/day (excessive fat gain rate)
- **Weight gain 0.25-0.5 kg/week** → Maintain current intake (optimal zone)
- **Recalculate TDEE every 5 kg gained** (more mass = higher expenditure)

# 6. PROTOCOL: TRAINING PROGRAMMING

## 6.1 Programming Principles

**Frequency**: Schoenfeld et al. (2016, _Sports Medicine_): Training each muscle ≥2x/week superior to 1x/week for hypertrophy. No significant difference between 2x and 3x when volume equated (2019 update, 25 studies).

**Recommended structure**: 3-day full body for months 1-6, transitioning to 4-day upper/lower split.

**Volume**: Schoenfeld et al. (2017, _J Sports Sciences_): Graded dose-response — each additional weekly set per muscle group increased effect size by 0.023. ~10 sets/muscle/week as starting point. Barbalho et al. (2019, _MSSE_, 40 trained women): 5-10 sets = equal or greater improvements vs 15-20 sets.

**Rep ranges**: Schoenfeld et al. (2017, 2021 IUSCA Position Stand): Hypertrophy occurs from ~30% to 85% 1RM. 8-15 reps most practical for beginners.

**Effort**: Refalo et al. (2022, _Sports Medicine_): 1-3 RIR (reps in reserve) = similar hypertrophy to failure with less fatigue. Start at RPE 7 (3 RIR), progress to RPE 8 (2 RIR).

**Progressive overload method — Double Progression**:

1. Work within rep range (e.g., 8-12)
2. When top of range hit on ALL sets with good form → increase weight (+2.5 kg upper body, +5 kg lower body)
3. Drop back to bottom of range with new weight
4. Repeat

## 6.2 Glute-Specific Training Science

**Hip thrust vs squat**: Contreras et al. (2015, _J Applied Biomechanics_): Hip thrust = 69-87% MVIC gluteus maximus activation vs 29-45% for squat. Plotkin et al. (2023, _Frontiers in Physiology_, first MRI-measured RCT): Gluteal hypertrophy was **similar** between hip thrust and squat groups over 9 weeks, while squat produced significantly more quadriceps and adductor growth. Krause Neto et al. (2025 meta-analysis): Moderate overall effect (SMD 0.71), recommended combining hip extension exercises.

**Gluteus medius (hip dips)**: Plotkin et al. found virtually **no growth** of gluteus medius from squats or hip thrusts alone. **Targeted hip abduction work is essential.** Moore et al. (2020, _IJSPT_): Highest activation exercises = side-lying hip abduction, hip hitch/pelvic drop, standing cable abduction, resisted side-steps.

**Agent rule**: For subjects with hip dips, ALWAYS include direct gluteus medius work (abduction exercises). Hip thrusts and squats alone will NOT address this.

## 6.3 Upper Body for Thin Frames

**Mind-muscle connection**: Schoenfeld et al. (2018, _EJSS_): Internal focus produced **12.4% biceps hypertrophy vs 6.9% with external focus**. Effect strongest with isolation exercises at moderate loads.

**Visual width strategy**: Prioritize lateral deltoid development (lateral raises, overhead press) and latissimus dorsi (lat pulldowns → pull-ups, rows) to create shoulder width that balances wide hips.

# 8. PROTOCOL: SLEEP & RECOVERY

## 8.1 Sleep Deprivation Impact on Muscle Growth

| Study                                        | Finding                                                                                                         |
| -------------------------------------------- | --------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Lamon et al. (2021, _Physiological Reports_) | Single night total sleep deprivation: MPS -18% (p=0.040), cortisol +21% (p=0.030), testosterone -24% (p=0.029)  |

## 8.2 Sleep Protocol

| Parameter           | Target                                        | Rationale                                               |
| ------------------- | --------------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------- |
| Duration            | 7-9 hours nightly                             | Non-negotiable for GH pulsatility and MPS               |

| Temperature         | 18-20°C bedroom                               | Facilitates slow-wave sleep onset                       |

<a id="section-9-hormones"></a>

# 9. PROTOCOL: HORMONAL OPTIMIZATION

## 9.1 The Caloric Surplus IS the Hormonal Intervention

For a woman at BMI 18.5, chronic energy insufficiency causes:

- HPG axis suppression → reduced estrogen, progesterone, testosterone
- Reduced T3 thyroid hormone → metabolism slowdown
- Elevated cortisol → catabolism
- Suppressed leptin below ~3 ng/mL → anovulation

**Mallinson et al. (2013)**: Simply increasing caloric intake restored T3, leptin, menstrual function within 23-74 days in amenorrheic women.

**Agent rule**: For underweight subjects, DO NOT recommend complex hormonal interventions. The primary intervention is achieving and sustaining caloric surplus.

## 9.2 Natural Optimization Levers

| Lever                                        | Mechanism                                   | Protocol                                                                                                                                                                                                              |
| -------------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------- | --------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Compound resistance training                 | Largest acute GH and testosterone responses | Already covered in training program                                                                                                                                                                                   |
| Sleep 7-9h                                   | Maximizes GH pulsatility                    | Already covered in sleep protocol                                                                                                                                                                                     |
| Stress management                            | Reduces catabolic cortisol                  | 10-20 min daily meditation/breathing. Multiple systematic reviews confirm cortisol reduction.                                                                                                                         |
| Sauna (if available)                         | Acute GH increases 2-16x                    | Leppäluoto et al. (1986). Effect habituates with repeated use.                                                                                                                                                        |
| **AVOID cold water immersion post-training** | Attenuates hypertrophy                      | Roberts et al. (2015, _J Physiol_): 12 weeks CWI = -13 percentage points quad mass gain + blunted satellite cell activation. Piñero et al. (2024, first meta-analysis): CWI likely attenuates RT-induced hypertrophy. |

<a id="section-10-neat"></a>

# 10. PROTOCOL: NEAT & LIFESTYLE MANAGEMENT

## 10.1 Why NEAT Matters for This Subject

NEAT is the primary mechanism of weight-gain resistance in "hardgainers" (Levine et al., 1999). It can vary by up to 2,000 kcal/day and increases unconsciously in response to overfeeding.

## 10.2 NEAT Management Rules

| Rule                                                     | Target                                              | Rationale                                                       |
| -------------------------------------------------------- | --------------------------------------------------- | --------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Daily steps                                              | 5,000-7,000/day max                                 | Enough for cardiovascular health without excessive caloric burn |
| Dedicated cardio                                         | Max 1-2 sessions of 15-20 min at low intensity/week | Higher volumes counteract surplus                               |
| Standing/movement jobs                                   | Add 200-300 kcal to daily target                    | Compensate for occupational NEAT                                |
| If weight not increasing despite hitting calorie targets | Add 200-300 kcal                                    | NEAT upregulation is the most likely culprit                    |

# 11. PROTOCOL: TRACKING & ADJUSTMENTS

## 11.1 Measurement Schedule

| Metric              | Frequency                              | Method                                                                | Notes                                            |
| ------------------- | -------------------------------------- | --------------------------------------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------ |
| Body weight         | Daily (morning, fasted, post-bathroom) | Scale                                                                 | Track WEEKLY AVERAGES, ignore daily fluctuations |
| Hip circumference   | Biweekly                               | Tape at widest point of glutes                                        | Primary progress marker                          |
| Thigh circumference | Biweekly                               | Tape at mid-thigh                                                     | Hamstring/quad growth marker                     |
| Arm circumference   | Biweekly                               | Tape at peak of flexed biceps                                         | Upper body progress                              |
| Waist circumference | Biweekly                               | Tape at navel                                                         | Monitor fat distribution                         |
| Progress photos     | Monthly                                | Same lighting, pose, time. Front, side, back.                         | Visual record                                    |
| Training log        | Every session                          | Exercises, weight, sets, reps                                         | Progressive overload tracking                    |
| Subjective markers  | Weekly                                 | Energy, sleep quality, mood, menstrual regularity, appetite, recovery | Holistic health monitoring                       |

## 11.2 Adjustment Decision Tree

```
Weekly average weight change:
├── < 0.25 kg/week for 2 consecutive weeks
│   → Increase daily calories by 200 kcal
│   → Check: Are you actually eating your target? (Most common issue)
│   → Check: Has NEAT increased? (Track steps)
│
├── 0.25-0.50 kg/week
│   → OPTIMAL — change nothing
│
├── 0.50-0.75 kg/week
│   → Acceptable — monitor body composition markers
│   → If waist growing faster than hips → slight surplus reduction (-100 kcal)
│
└── > 0.75 kg/week
    → Reduce daily calories by 150 kcal
    → Likely excessive fat gain rate

Training stall (same weight/reps for 3 consecutive sessions):
├── Check recovery: sleep, stress, calories
├── Change exercise variation
├── Add 1 set to the stalled muscle group
└── If persistent → deload week (50% volume, same intensity)
```

## 11.3 Optional Advanced Tracking

**DEXA scan** every 3-6 months if available. Gold standard for tracking muscle vs fat gain and regional distribution.

**Recalculate TDEE** every 5 kg gained — more mass = higher expenditure = need more calories to maintain surplus.

- Upper body gains matching lower body (women gain lower-body muscle faster than upper-body per systematic review — Stec et al., 2023, _PLOS ONE_)

# AGENT CONFIGURATION NOTES

## Decision Priority Hierarchy

When advising this subject, prioritize interventions in this order:

1. **Caloric surplus** (most impactful, most likely failure point)
2. **Protein adequacy and distribution** (second most impactful)
3. **Training consistency and progressive overload** (third)
4. **Sleep quality and duration** (fourth)
5. **Supplementation** (fifth — enhances but doesn't replace above)
6. **Timing optimization, cycle periodization, etc.** (marginal — only after 1-5 are solid)

## Common Failure Modes to Monitor

1. **Not actually eating enough** — most common issue by far. Subject likely underestimates intake.
2. **NEAT compensation** — unconscious movement increase negating surplus
3. **Inconsistent training attendance** — missing sessions ruins progressive overload
4. **Sleep deprivation** — single largest recovery variable after nutrition
5. **Program hopping** — switching programs too frequently prevents progressive overload
6. **Fear of fat gain** — may unconsciously limit food intake; address with fat distribution science
7. **Neglecting gluteus medius work** — hip dips will not resolve without direct abduction training

## Response Templates

**When user reports no weight gain**: "Let's check the fundamentals first. Are you consistently hitting 2,500 kcal/day? The most common reason for stalled weight gain is caloric intake being lower than you think. Try tracking everything you eat for 3 days — most hardgainers find they're eating 300-500 kcal less than they estimated."

**When user reports rapid weight gain (>0.75 kg/week)**: "Great that the scale is moving! But gaining too fast means a larger proportion goes to fat rather than muscle. Let's pull back by 150 kcal/day and aim for the 0.25-0.5 kg/week sweet spot."

**When user asks about cardio**: "For your goal, limit cardio to 1-2 short sessions of 15-20 minutes at low intensity per week. Your priority is creating a caloric surplus, and excessive cardio directly opposes that. Walking 5,000-7,000 steps/day is enough for cardiovascular health."

**When user is frustrated with slow visible results**: "Real muscle hypertrophy typically becomes visible after 6-8 weeks minimum. In the first months, most gains are neural (your brain learning to recruit muscles) and compositional (glycogen, water, early fat). The visual transformation accelerates around months 3-4. Trust the process — the science is unambiguous that untrained women respond to progressive overload."
